On the Necessity of Rereading

20 November 2025

We treat reading as if it were archaeology—dig once, extract the meaning, move on. The book has been conquered; its secrets are now ours. This is, of course, completely wrong.

Rereading is not repetition. It's not the literary equivalent of watching your favorite movie for the hundredth time, mouthing along with the dialogue. When you return to a book you've already read, you're not returning at all. You're arriving somewhere else.

The text hasn't changed—this is the trick. We assume that because the words are the same, the experience must be the same. But you've changed. You're reading with different eyes, a different history, different questions. The book that bored you at twenty might arrest you at forty. The passage that seemed clear ten years ago might suddenly reveal itself as deeply strange.

What the Ancients Knew

The ancient world understood this. Roman education wasn't about coverage—it was about depth. Students read the same texts over and over: Homer, Virgil, Cicero. Not because they had a limited library, but because they understood that mastery requires return.

Seneca puts it directly: "You should be extending your stay among writers whose genius is unquestionable, deriving constant nourishment from them if you wish to gain anything from your reading that will find a lasting place in your mind."

"Everywhere means nowhere. When a person spends all his time in foreign travel, he ends by having many acquaintances, but no friends."

He's talking about books. Stay with them. Return to them. The modern anxiety about "keeping up" with new releases would have seemed bizarre to a Roman reader. Why rush through twenty books when you haven't truly read one?

The Problem with Reading Culture

Contemporary reading culture is about accumulation. We track books read per year. We maintain to-read lists that grow faster than we can shrink them. We talk about "getting through" books, as if reading were a form of transit rather than dwelling.

This is partly a market problem—publishers need to sell new books—but it's also a status problem. Reading functions as cultural capital, and cultural capital requires display. Rereading doesn't count. No one is impressed when you tell them you read Middlemarch again.

But Middlemarch rewards rereading in ways that most contemporary fiction can't. Not because Eliot is necessarily a better writer than anyone working today, but because she's writing with that kind of density in mind. The book is constructed to be reread.

What Changes When You Reread

First reading: plot. What happens next? You're following the story, tracking the characters, assembling the world.

Second reading: structure. How does this work? Now you can see the architecture, the callbacks, the careful placement of themes and images.

Third reading: language. The actual sentences. The rhythm of the prose. The precision of word choice. All the things you miss when you're just trying to find out what happens.

This isn't always true—some books don't repay rereading because they never offered much in the first place. But the books that last, the ones that remain in circulation decades or centuries after publication, tend to be built for return.

A Different Kind of Reading

Rereading is also more honest. First-time readers are tourists, interpreting everything through the lens of their own moment and assumptions. Rereaders start to see the text on its own terms.

This is especially important with old books. The first time through Thucydides, you're mostly confused. The second time, you start to understand how Greek historical thought actually works, rather than imposing modern historiographical expectations on an ancient text.

It's slower. It's inefficient. It doesn't help you win arguments at parties about books you've technically read. But if the goal is actually understanding rather than coverage, there's no substitute.

So: read less. Reread more. Stay with the books that resist you. The ones you don't fully understand the first time through are usually the ones worth reading twice.
